What Is Lower Cross Syndrome?

Lower cross syndrome (LCS) is a pattern of muscle imbalances affecting the lumbar spine, pelvis, and hips. Like its upper-body counterpart, LCS involves a predictable combination of overactive/tight and underactive/weak muscle groups that create postural distortion and movement dysfunction.

The pattern typically involves:

  • Tight/overactive: hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris) and lumbar extensors (erector spinae)
  • Weak/underactive: gluteal muscles and abdominals

The result is often anterior pelvic tilt — where the front of the pelvis tilts forward — creating an exaggerated lumbar lordosis and increased mechanical stress on the lower back.

Lower cross syndrome is extremely common in people who sit for long periods, and it's a frequently overlooked contributor to chronic lower back pain. Addressing the pattern — not just treating symptoms — may lead to more lasting improvement.

How Lower Cross Syndrome Contributes to Pain

  • Chronic low back aching from sustained lumbar compression
  • Hip flexor pain or tightness — especially after sitting or on exertion
  • Gluteal weakness affecting walking, running, and single-leg activities
  • Sacroiliac joint dysfunction from pelvic instability
  • Increased risk of sciatica from altered lumbar mechanics

Our Approach

Common Questions

Lower Cross Syndrome FAQs

What is lower cross syndrome?

Lower cross syndrome describes a pattern where hip flexors and lumbar extensors are tight while gluteal muscles and abdominals are weak. This imbalance can contribute to anterior pelvic tilt and low back pain.

Can lower cross syndrome be improved?

Improvement is possible with a combination of chiropractic care, soft tissue work, and corrective exercise targeting the specific imbalances. Progress depends on consistency and individual factors.

Is lower cross syndrome related to anterior pelvic tilt?

Yes. Anterior pelvic tilt is a common postural result of the lower cross syndrome muscle imbalance pattern.
